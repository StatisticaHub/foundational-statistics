What is statistics?

Mr. R · Prof Stat · ~14 min · No prerequisites
📍 Scene — a university statistics classroom, first day
EM
Emma

Everyone keeps telling me statistics is important. But honestly, what even is it? I feel like I already know what an average is.

MR
Mr. R

That is a fair starting point. You do know what an average is. But statistics is the science of going from that average to a conclusion you can actually trust — and being honest about how uncertain that conclusion is.

OL
Oliver

What do you mean uncertain? If I calculate an average from data, it is just a number. What is uncertain about it?

MR
Mr. R

Imagine you measure the blood pressure of 50 patients and compute the mean. You want to say something about all patients, not just those 50. The moment you generalise beyond your data, you introduce uncertainty. Statistics gives you the tools to quantify that uncertainty honestly.

The two branches

Statistics divides into two large families of ideas that work together:

Descriptive statistics

Summarises and describes the data you actually have. Means, medians, standard deviations, charts, tables. No inference beyond the data itself.

Inferential statistics

Uses sample data to draw conclusions about a broader population. Includes estimation, confidence intervals, and hypothesis testing.

JA
James

So if I survey 200 students about sleep hours and report the mean, that is descriptive. But if I claim something about all university students based on those 200, that is inferential?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly right. And the inferential step is where most mistakes in published research happen — because people forget that the claim about all students depends on how those 200 were chosen.

Why statistics matters in research

Consider these three scenarios. In each one, what looks simple on the surface hides a statistical question:

ScenarioDescriptive questionInferential question
Clinical trialWhat was the average improvement in the treatment group?Does the drug work in all patients like these?
EducationWhat is the mean exam score in this cohort?Does the teaching method improve results generally?
EpidemiologyHow many cases were observed this week?Is the disease spreading faster than before?

A common misconception

SO
Sophia

My cousin says statistics can prove anything. Is that true?

MR
Mr. R

Statistics cannot prove anything in the mathematical sense. It quantifies evidence. A well-designed study gives you strong evidence. A poorly designed study can mislead — but that is an abuse of statistics, not a feature of it.

EM
Emma

So the tool is not the problem. How it is used is?

MR
Mr. R

Precisely. Which is why understanding statistics properly is important — not to manipulate data, but to read other people's claims critically and build your own work on solid foundations.

Important distinction

Statistics works with data, not with individual facts. A single observation tells you almost nothing. Patterns across many observations tell you something meaningful — if collected and analysed correctly.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Statistics is the science of learning from data under uncertainty.
  • Descriptive statistics summarises existing data; inferential statistics generalises beyond it.
  • The inferential leap always introduces uncertainty — statistics quantifies that honestly.
  • Poor study design, not statistics itself, leads to misleading conclusions.
Detailed notes

What is statistics?

1. Formal definition

Statistics is the discipline concerned with the collection, organisation, analysis, interpretation, and presentation of data. It provides principled methods for drawing conclusions from incomplete information — the situation that characterises almost all real-world enquiry.

Definition — statistics

The science of learning from data, including the design of data collection, the analysis of data, and the communication of results with appropriate measures of uncertainty.

2. Descriptive statistics

Descriptive statistics concerns itself entirely with the data at hand. No generalisation is made. The goal is to summarise, compress, and display the data in a way that makes it understandable.

The main tools of descriptive statistics are:

  • Measures of centre: mean, median, mode
  • Measures of spread: range, variance, standard deviation, interquartile range
  • Measures of shape: skewness, kurtosis
  • Graphical summaries: histograms, boxplots, bar charts, scatter plots
  • Tabular summaries: frequency tables, contingency tables
Example

A sample of 10 patients has resting heart rates (bpm): 68, 72, 70, 74, 69, 75, 71, 73, 68, 70. The sample mean is x̄ = 71.0 bpm. This is a descriptive statement about these 10 patients only.

3. Inferential statistics

Inferential statistics uses data from a sample to draw conclusions about a population. This is the dominant activity in research — we almost never have access to the full population.

Population vs sample

Population: the entire collection of objects or individuals of interest.
Sample: a subset of the population that we actually observe and measure.

The inferential leap involves three core activities:

  1. Estimation: Using sample statistics to estimate population parameters (e.g. using x̄ to estimate μ).
  2. Confidence intervals: Constructing a range of plausible values for a parameter, accounting for sampling variability.
  3. Hypothesis testing: Evaluating evidence for or against a specific claim about a population.

4. Parameters vs statistics

A critical notational distinction that will appear throughout this course:

QuantityPopulation (parameter)Sample (statistic)
Meanμ (mu)x̄ (x-bar)
Varianceσ² (sigma squared)s²
Standard deviationσ (sigma)s
Proportionpp̂ (p-hat)
Correlationρ (rho)r
Regression slopeβ (beta)b or β̂

Parameters describe the population and are usually unknown. Statistics describe the sample and are computed from data. The fundamental problem of inferential statistics is to use the known (statistics) to make principled statements about the unknown (parameters).

5. Derivation — why the sample mean is a good estimator of μ

One of the first results in statistical theory is that x̄ is an unbiased estimator of μ. This means that on average, across repeated samples, x̄ equals μ. Here is the derivation:

Proof: E[x̄] = μ (unbiasedness of the sample mean)
1
x̄ = (1/n) · Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ xᵢ
The sample mean is the sum of all n observations divided by n.
2
E[x̄] = E[(1/n) · Σᵢ xᵢ]
Take the expectation of both sides. E[·] denotes expected value — the long-run average over repeated samples.
3
E[x̄] = (1/n) · Σᵢ E[xᵢ]
Linearity of expectation: E[aX + bY] = aE[X] + bE[Y]. The constant 1/n can be factored out; E distributes over sums.
4
E[x̄] = (1/n) · Σᵢ μ = (1/n) · n·μ
Each xᵢ is drawn from the population with mean μ, so E[xᵢ] = μ for all i. Summing n copies of μ gives n·μ.
5
∴ E[x̄] = μ ✓
The n's cancel. The sample mean is an unbiased estimator of the population mean — it hits the right target on average.

6. The role of uncertainty

A critical idea that separates statistical thinking from naive data reading is the acknowledgement of sampling variability. Any statistic computed from a sample will differ from sample to sample, simply because different subsets of a population are observed.

Sampling variability

The natural variation in a statistic (e.g. x̄) from one sample to another, when repeated samples are drawn from the same population. It is not an error — it is the fundamental reality of sampling.

The standard deviation of the sampling distribution of x̄ is called the standard error:

SE(x̄) = σ / √n

This tells us: larger samples produce less variable estimates. The uncertainty shrinks as n grows — specifically, it shrinks proportionally to 1/√n, not 1/n. Doubling the sample size reduces the standard error by a factor of √2 ≈ 1.41, not by half.

7. Branches of statistical methodology

BranchCore questionExamples

PredictiveWhat will happen?Regression, classification models
CausalWhy does it happen?Experiments, causal models, DAGs
Note — prediction vs causal inference

A model can predict accurately without identifying the cause. Blood pressure may predict cardiac events because it is correlated with the underlying pathology, not because it is the cause. Understanding this distinction is fundamental to interpreting statistical results correctly.

Practice exercises

What is statistics?

Five exercises ranging from conceptual to applied. Reveal the answer only after attempting each question.

Exercise 1
Descriptive or inferential?
Easy +

For each statement below, identify whether it is a descriptive or inferential conclusion:

  1. The average waiting time for the 45 patients surveyed was 23.4 minutes.
  2. Based on a sample of 500 adults, we estimate that 62% of UK adults exercise fewer than 150 minutes per week.
  3. In this dataset, systolic blood pressure was positively correlated with BMI (r = 0.41).
  4. The new medication reduces blood pressure in patients with hypertension.
Answer
  1. Descriptive. This reports a computed value for the observed sample only. No generalisation is made.
  2. Inferential. The word "estimate" and the phrase "UK adults" (population) signal a generalisation beyond the 500 sampled.
  3. Descriptive. r = 0.41 is a statistic computed from this dataset. No claim about a broader population is made here.
  4. Inferential. "Patients with hypertension" is a population. The claim extends beyond the trial participants.
Exercise 2
Parameters and statistics
Easy +

A researcher measures fasting glucose (mmol/L) in a sample of 80 adults from a city of 200,000. She computes a mean of 5.2 mmol/L and a standard deviation of 0.9 mmol/L.

  1. What is the population in this study?
  2. What is the sample?
  3. Is 5.2 mmol/L a parameter or a statistic? What symbol would we use?
  4. What symbol represents the unknown population mean?
Answer
  1. The population is all 200,000 adults in the city.
  2. The sample consists of the 80 adults who were measured.
  3. 5.2 mmol/L is a statistic — it was computed from the sample. We write it as x̄ = 5.2.
  4. The unknown population mean is denoted μ (mu). It is the true average glucose across all 200,000 adults, which we do not know.
Exercise 3
Standard error calculation
Medium +

Assume the population standard deviation of systolic blood pressure is σ = 18 mmHg.

  1. A cardiologist samples n = 36 patients. What is the standard error of the sample mean?
  2. She increases the sample to n = 144. What is the new standard error?
  3. By what factor did the standard error decrease when n quadrupled?
  4. How large a sample is needed to achieve SE ≤ 1.5 mmHg?
Answer
  1. SE = σ/√n = 18/√36 = 18/6 = 3.0 mmHg
  2. SE = 18/√144 = 18/12 = 1.5 mmHg
  3. The SE halved (factor of 2). When n quadruples, SE divides by √4 = 2. This illustrates the 1/√n relationship.
  4. We need SE ≤ 1.5, so σ/√n ≤ 1.5 → √n ≥ 18/1.5 = 12 → n ≥ 144. A minimum of 144 patients is required.
Exercise 4
Identifying statistical goals
Medium +

A clinical researcher conducts a randomised controlled trial testing whether Drug A reduces LDL cholesterol more than a placebo. At the end of the trial, she observes a mean LDL reduction of 12.4 mg/dL in the treatment group (n=85) and 3.1 mg/dL in the placebo group (n=82).

  1. Write one purely descriptive statement based on these results.
  2. Write one inferential statement based on these results.
  3. What additional information would you need before making a confident inferential claim?
Answer
  1. "In this trial, the mean LDL reduction was 12.4 mg/dL in the treatment group and 3.1 mg/dL in the placebo group — a difference of 9.3 mg/dL."
  2. "Drug A appears to reduce LDL more effectively than placebo in patients with this profile." (This generalises to a broader patient population.)
  3. To make a confident inferential claim you would need: a p-value or confidence interval for the difference, measures of variability (standard deviations), confirmation that the randomisation was properly conducted, and ideally external validation in a separate population.
Exercise 5
Proof — unbiasedness of x̄
Hard +

Let x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ be independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random variables each with mean μ and variance σ². Define x̄ = (1/n)Σxᵢ.

  1. Show that E[x̄] = μ.
  2. Show that Var(x̄) = σ²/n.
  3. Hence write the standard error of x̄.
  4. What does it mean in practical terms that Var(x̄) → 0 as n → ∞?
Answer

(a) E[x̄] = E[(1/n)Σxᵢ] = (1/n)·ΣE[xᵢ] = (1/n)·nμ = μ. ✓ (Linearity of expectation.)

(b) Var(x̄) = Var((1/n)Σxᵢ) = (1/n²)·ΣVar(xᵢ) = (1/n²)·nσ² = σ²/n. ✓ (Independence allows variances to add; constants square when taken outside Var.)

(c) SE(x̄) = √Var(x̄) = σ/√n.

(d) As n → ∞, Var(x̄) → 0, meaning x̄ converges to μ in probability. With a very large sample, the sample mean is essentially the same as the population mean — the uncertainty in the estimate vanishes. This property is called consistency.
