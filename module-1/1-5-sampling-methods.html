Sampling methods and bias

Mr. R · Emma · Oliver · James · Sophia · ~24 min · Module 1 final lesson
📍 Scene — the class plans a health survey for a university campus
EM
Emma

We have learned about populations, samples, variables, tables and graphs. So for our campus health survey, can we just ask whoever is available outside the library?

MR
Mr. R

You could, but then your data may describe library-area students more than the whole campus. A convenient sample is easy to collect, but it can quietly distort the story.

OL
Oliver

But if we ask 500 students, surely the sample becomes reliable because it is large?

MR
Mr. R

Large is not the same as representative. A large biased sample can be worse than a smaller well-designed sample because it gives false confidence.

The core idea

A sample is useful only when it can stand in for the population in a meaningful way. Sampling is therefore not a small administrative step before analysis. It is part of the statistical argument itself.

Sampling method

A rule or procedure used to choose which units from a population will be included in the sample.

Bias warning

If the sampling method systematically includes some kinds of units more than others, the sample may give a misleading picture even if the calculations are correct.

JA
James

So bias is not just about someone lying or changing the data?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. Bias can appear before a single number is analysed. It can enter through who was invited, who responded, who was excluded, how questions were asked, and when measurements were taken.

Five common sampling methods

MethodMain ideaStrengthMain risk
Simple random samplingEvery unit has an equal chance of selectionClean theoretical basisNeeds a complete sampling frame
Systematic samplingSelect every kth unit after a random startEasy to implementPeriodic patterns can bias results
Stratified samplingSample separately within important subgroupsEnsures subgroup representationRequires correct strata information
Cluster samplingRandomly select natural groups, then sample within themPractical for spread-out populationsLess precise if clusters are internally similar
Convenience samplingUse units that are easiest to reachFast and cheapUsually weak for generalisation
SO
Sophia

Stratified sampling sounds more careful. Is it always better?

MR
Mr. R

It is better when the strata are meaningful. If year of study, sex, or department affects the outcome, stratifying by those variables can prevent important groups from disappearing in the sample.

EM
Emma

And cluster sampling is different because we choose groups first?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. For example, instead of sampling individual pupils across an entire country, we might randomly select schools first and then collect data from pupils inside those selected schools.

Sampling is about credibility

When you read a report saying “42% of students are sleep-deprived”, the first question should not be the percentage formula. The first question should be: “Which students were sampled, and who was missed?”

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Sampling design determines how credible a descriptive or inferential claim is.
  • A large sample can still be biased if it is collected poorly.
  • Random sampling reduces selection bias, but requires a clear sampling frame.
  • Stratified sampling protects subgroup representation; cluster sampling improves practicality.
  • Bias can enter through selection, non-response, coverage, measurement and survivorship.
Detailed notes

Sampling methods and bias

These notes are intentionally in-depth because sampling is the bridge between descriptive statistics and trustworthy statistical reasoning. In descriptive statistics, we summarise the data we have. But even a descriptive summary can be misleading if the data were collected from the wrong people, places, records or time periods.

1. Why sampling matters

In most real situations, we cannot measure every member of a population. A university may have thousands of students, a city may have millions of residents, and a health system may contain decades of patient records. Sampling provides a practical way to collect information from a subset.

Definition — sample

A sample is a subset of units selected from a population for observation, measurement or analysis.

Definition — representative sample

A representative sample is one whose composition reflects the important characteristics of the target population closely enough for the purpose of the study.

Representativeness does not mean the sample must be a perfect miniature copy of the population. It means the sample should not systematically over-represent or under-represent groups in a way that distorts the descriptive conclusion.

2. Target population, accessible population and sampling frame

A common beginner mistake is to say “the population is everyone” without specifying who can actually be studied. In statistical work, three layers should be separated:

ConceptMeaningExample
Target populationThe full group we want to describe or understandAll undergraduate students at a university
Accessible populationThe part of the target population that can realistically be reachedStudents currently enrolled and contactable by university email
Sampling frameThe actual list or mechanism from which the sample is chosenA student registry exported from the university database

If the sampling frame does not cover the target population properly, coverage bias may occur. For example, an online survey excludes people without internet access; a hospital-record study excludes people who never attend hospital.

3. Simple random sampling

In simple random sampling, every unit in the sampling frame has the same probability of being chosen. If there are \(N\) units in the population and we choose \(n\) units, each unit has probability:

P(selected) = n / N

The main advantage is conceptual clarity. Random selection breaks the link between the researcher’s preferences and the selected units. It helps prevent the sample from being shaped by convenience, judgement or hidden preference.

Limitation

Simple random sampling requires a complete and accurate sampling frame. Without a list of all units, equal probability sampling may be impossible.

4. Systematic sampling

In systematic sampling, units are arranged in a list, a random starting point is chosen, and then every kth unit is selected. If the population size is \(N\) and the desired sample size is \(n\), the sampling interval is approximately:

k = N / n

For example, if a clinic has 1,000 appointment records and a researcher wants 100 records, she may choose every 10th record after a random start between 1 and 10.

The danger is periodicity. If the list has a repeating pattern related to the outcome, systematic sampling can accidentally select a biased pattern. For instance, choosing every 7th day in a hospital may always select the same weekday, missing weekend effects.

5. Stratified sampling

In stratified sampling, the population is divided into non-overlapping subgroups called strata, and a sample is taken from each stratum. Strata are usually based on variables that are important to the study, such as sex, year group, age band, department, region, or disease severity.

Definition — stratum

A stratum is a subgroup of the population formed before sampling, usually because that subgroup is important for representation or analysis.

There are two common versions:

TypeHow it worksWhen useful
Proportionate stratified samplingSample from each stratum in proportion to its population sizeWhen we want the sample composition to mirror the population
Disproportionate stratified samplingOversample smaller strata deliberatelyWhen small groups need enough observations for description or comparison

Suppose a university has 60% undergraduate students and 40% postgraduate students. In a proportionate stratified sample of 200, we would sample 120 undergraduates and 80 postgraduates.

n_h = (N_h / N) × n

Here, \(N_h\) is the population size of stratum \(h\), \(N\) is the total population size, and \(n\) is the total sample size.

6. Cluster sampling

Cluster sampling is used when the population is naturally divided into groups, such as schools, hospitals, classes, villages, households or GP practices. Instead of sampling individuals directly across the entire population, we sample clusters first.

Cluster sampling is especially useful when a full list of individuals is unavailable, but a list of clusters is available. For example, it may be easier to sample 20 schools and then survey pupils within those schools than to sample pupils from every school in a country.

Precision warning

Cluster samples are often less statistically efficient than simple random samples of the same size because units inside the same cluster tend to be similar. Pupils in the same school may share environment, teaching style and socioeconomic context.

This similarity within clusters is called intra-cluster correlation. If cluster members are very similar, a sample of 500 pupils from 5 schools may contain less information than 500 pupils spread across 100 schools.

7. Convenience, voluntary and purposive sampling

Not all sampling methods are probability-based. In many student projects, pilot studies and online surveys, samples are chosen because they are easy to access.

MethodDescriptionMain caution
Convenience samplingSelect people or records that are easiest to reachHigh risk of selection bias
Voluntary response samplingParticipants choose themselves into the samplePeople with strong opinions may be over-represented
Purposive samplingResearcher deliberately selects information-rich casesUseful qualitatively, but weak for population percentages
Snowball samplingParticipants recruit further participantsUseful for hidden populations, but network bias is likely

These approaches are not automatically useless. They can be appropriate for exploratory work, qualitative research, feasibility studies and early pilots. The problem arises when results from such samples are written as if they represent a wider population.

8. Types of sampling bias

Bias is a systematic distortion. Random error makes results noisy; bias pushes results in a particular direction. Increasing sample size reduces random error, but it does not automatically remove bias.

Bias typeMeaningExample
Selection biasThe selection process favours certain unitsSurveying only gym users to estimate student fitness
Coverage biasThe sampling frame misses part of the target populationUsing email lists when some students do not use university email
Non-response biasSelected units do not respond, and responders differ from non-respondersStressed students may ignore a wellbeing survey
Volunteer biasPeople who opt in differ from those who do notHealth-conscious people join a diet study
Survivorship biasOnly successful or remaining units are observedStudying only companies that still exist
Measurement biasThe way data are measured systematically distorts valuesA leading question makes symptoms sound more common

9. Random error versus bias

Random error is unpredictable variation caused by chance. Bias is a systematic departure from the truth. This distinction is central to statistical thinking:

Observed estimate = True value + Bias + Random error

A small sample from a good sampling process may be imprecise but not systematically wrong. A huge sample from a biased process may be precise but wrong. This is why sampling design comes before statistical calculation.

10. Descriptive writing from sampled data

When writing descriptive conclusions, always match the wording to the sampling method. Do not overclaim.

Sampling situationWeak wordingBetter wording
Convenience survey outside library“Students sleep 6.1 hours on average.”“Among students surveyed outside the library, the mean reported sleep duration was 6.1 hours.”
Random sample from university registry“Our sample had 38% first years.”“In the random sample of registered students, 38% were first years.”
Online voluntary response poll“Most students oppose the policy.”“Among students who chose to respond to the online poll, 64% opposed the policy.”

11. Checklist for evaluating a sample

  1. What is the target population?
  2. What is the accessible population?
  3. What sampling frame was used?
  4. Was selection random, systematic, stratified, clustered or convenient?
  5. Who might have been excluded?
  6. Who might be less likely to respond?
  7. Are important subgroups represented?
  8. Is the conclusion worded only as strongly as the design allows?
Module 1 final summary
  • Statistics begins with defining the population, sample and variables clearly.
  • Tables and graphs summarise data, but sampling determines what those summaries can mean.
  • Descriptive statistics should always be written with careful scope: who was actually observed?
  • Good statistical analysis cannot fully rescue poorly collected data.

Practice exercises

Sampling methods and bias

These exercises move from recognition to interpretation and study critique.

Exercise 1
Identify the sampling method
Easy+

Identify the sampling method used in each case: (a) every 20th name from a list; (b) 50 students randomly chosen from each faculty; (c) five schools randomly chosen, then all pupils in those schools surveyed; (d) people stopped outside a shopping centre.

Answer
  1. Systematic sampling.
  2. Stratified sampling, because faculty defines strata.
  3. Cluster sampling, because schools are selected as natural groups.
  4. Convenience sampling.
Exercise 2
Calculate stratum sample sizes
Medium+

A university has 4,000 students: 2,400 undergraduates, 1,200 taught postgraduates and 400 research postgraduates. You need a proportionate stratified sample of 200. How many should be selected from each group?

Answer

Use n_h = (N_h / N) × n.

  • Undergraduates: (2400/4000)×200 = 120
  • Taught postgraduates: (1200/4000)×200 = 60
  • Research postgraduates: (400/4000)×200 = 20
Exercise 3
Bias in a wellbeing survey
Medium+

A wellbeing survey is emailed to all students. Only 8% respond. The report states: “Students at this university have high wellbeing.” Explain the main problem and rewrite the conclusion more carefully.

Answer

The main problem is non-response bias. The 8% who replied may differ systematically from the 92% who did not. Students with low wellbeing may ignore surveys, or students with strong experiences may be more likely to respond.

A better conclusion: “Among students who responded to the wellbeing survey, reported wellbeing was high. Because the response rate was 8%, this may not represent the whole student population.”

Exercise 4
Design a sample
Hard+

You want to estimate average weekly study hours among all students in a university. Design a sampling plan that is more credible than standing outside the library. Include population, sampling frame, method, and one likely bias that may remain.

Answer

Target population: all currently enrolled students. Sampling frame: current student registry. Method: stratified random sampling by level of study and faculty, with sample sizes proportionate to the population. Invite selected students and send reminders to reduce non-response.

Remaining bias: non-response bias may remain if students with very high workload or low engagement are less likely to complete the survey.

Exercise 5
Large sample but wrong conclusion
Hard+

An online poll receives 25,000 responses and reports that 78% of respondents support a policy. Explain why the large sample size does not automatically make the result representative.

Answer

The sample is large, but it is likely a voluntary response sample. People who know about the poll, have internet access, care strongly about the policy, and choose to respond may not represent the target population. Large n reduces random error around the respondent group, but it does not remove volunteer bias or coverage bias. The correct description is “78% of respondents supported the policy,” not necessarily “78% of the population supported the policy.”
