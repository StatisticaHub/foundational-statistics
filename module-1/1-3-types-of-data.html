Types of data and measurement scales

Mr. R · Emma, Oliver, James, Sophia · ~16 min · Foundation level
📍 Scene — the same classroom, now looking at a messy research spreadsheet
EM
Emma

I thought data just meant numbers in a spreadsheet. But this sheet has age, gender, blood group, pain score, income band, and treatment group. Are all of these really the same kind of data?

MR
Mr. R

They are all data, but they are not the same type of data. Before we calculate anything, we must ask what kind of variable each column represents.

OL
Oliver

Why does that matter? If we can put it into a spreadsheet, surely we can summarise it somehow.

MR
Mr. R

We can summarise it, yes. But the correct summary depends on the type. Taking an average of age makes sense. Taking an average of blood group does not.

The first split: categorical or numerical

The most important first question is whether a variable places observations into groups or records a measured amount.

Categorical variable

A variable whose values are labels or groups. Examples: blood group, treatment arm, disease status, smoking category, eye colour.

Numerical variable

A variable whose values are numbers representing counts or measurements. Examples: height, age, systolic blood pressure, number of hospital visits.

JA
James

So if a variable uses numbers, it must be numerical?

MR
Mr. R

Not always. That is a common trap. A hospital ward coded as 1, 2, 3, and 4 is still categorical, because the numbers are just labels. The arithmetic meaning matters, not the appearance.

Nominal, ordinal, interval, and ratio

Measurement scales tell us how much mathematical meaning the values have. This affects what summaries, graphs, and statistical methods are sensible.

ScaleMeaningExampleSafe summaries
NominalCategories with no natural orderBlood group, city, treatment armCounts, percentages, mode
OrdinalCategories with a natural orderPain: mild/moderate/severeCounts, percentages, median category
IntervalEqual units, no true zeroTemperature in CelsiusDifferences, mean, SD
RatioEqual units and true zeroWeight, age, income, timeAll arithmetic summaries

Discrete and continuous variables

SO
Sophia

Where do counts fit? Like number of GP visits or number of siblings?

MR
Mr. R

Counts are numerical, but they are discrete. They jump in whole numbers. Height and blood pressure are continuous in principle, because they can vary along a measurement scale.

Important warning

Do not choose a statistical method only because a variable contains numbers. First decide what the numbers mean. Are they labels, ranks, counts, or measurements?

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Variables can be categorical or numerical.
  • Categorical variables may be nominal or ordinal.
  • Numerical variables may be discrete or continuous.
  • Measurement scale determines which summaries and graphs are appropriate.
  • Numbers can be labels, so always interpret the meaning of the variable.
Detailed descriptive notes

Types of data and measurement scales

1. Why data type matters

Descriptive statistics begins with recognising the type of data being described. A variable's type controls how it should be summarised, displayed, interpreted, and later analysed. A poor choice at this stage can make the entire statistical description misleading.

Core principle

The correct descriptive summary depends on the meaning of the variable, not merely on whether its values look like words or numbers.

For example, the values 1, 2, and 3 may represent actual counts, ordered severity categories, or arbitrary ID codes. In the first case arithmetic is meaningful; in the second case order is meaningful but distances may not be; in the third case neither arithmetic nor order has statistical meaning.

2. Categorical variables

A categorical variable records membership of a group. Its values are categories rather than measured quantities. The basic descriptive tools for categorical data are frequency counts, relative frequencies, percentages, and sometimes the mode.

Relative frequency of category A = count in category A / total sample size
Percentage in category A = (count in category A / total sample size) × 100

Nominal variables

Nominal variables have categories with no natural order. Examples include blood group, country, treatment arm, eye colour, disease subtype, and hospital department. Because the categories are unordered, it is not meaningful to compute a mean, median, range, or standard deviation.

Descriptive summaries for nominal variables

Use counts, percentages, mode, bar charts, and pie charts if the number of categories is small. Avoid means, medians, and arithmetic comparisons.

Ordinal variables

Ordinal variables have categories with a meaningful order, but the gaps between adjacent categories are not necessarily equal. Examples include pain severity (none, mild, moderate, severe), education level, Likert-scale agreement, cancer stage, and satisfaction ratings.

Ordinal data can be ranked. It makes sense to say that severe pain is greater than moderate pain, but it is usually unsafe to say that the distance from mild to moderate is exactly the same as the distance from moderate to severe.

Descriptive summaries for ordinal variables

Use ordered frequency tables, percentages, cumulative percentages, median category, and stacked bar charts. Means may be reported for some rating scales, but only with a clear warning that the scale is being treated approximately as numerical.

3. Numerical variables

A numerical variable records a quantity. Arithmetic operations such as addition, subtraction, and averaging usually have meaning. Numerical variables are described using measures of centre, spread, and shape.

Descriptive featureCommon summariesWhat it tells us
CentreMean, medianTypical or central value
SpreadRange, IQR, variance, SDHow variable the observations are
ShapeSkewness, modality, outliersHow values are distributed
PositionPercentiles, quartiles, z-scoresWhere a value lies relative to others

Discrete numerical variables

A discrete numerical variable takes separate, countable values. Counts are the most common example. Number of children, number of hospital visits, number of adverse events, and number of correct answers are all discrete.

Discrete data can be summarised by counts and percentages when there are few possible values, or by mean and standard deviation when the count range is wide and the numerical magnitude matters.

Continuous numerical variables

A continuous variable can, in principle, take any value within an interval. Height, weight, blood pressure, time, temperature, glucose concentration, and cholesterol level are treated as continuous measurements.

In practice, continuous variables are rounded by measurement instruments. A recorded blood pressure of 122 mmHg does not mean blood pressure can only exist in whole numbers; it means the measurement has been rounded to the nearest unit.

4. Measurement scales

The traditional measurement scale framework is useful because it tells us what mathematical operations are meaningful.

ScaleOrder?Equal gaps?True zero?ExampleMeaningful operation
NominalNoNoNoBlood groupSame/different
OrdinalYesNoNoDisease stageRank comparison
IntervalYesYesNoCelsius temperatureDifferences
RatioYesYesYesWeightRatios and differences

Interval scale

An interval scale has equal units but no true zero. Temperature in Celsius is the standard example. The difference between 20°C and 30°C is the same size as the difference between 30°C and 40°C, but 40°C is not twice as hot as 20°C because zero Celsius is not the absence of temperature.

Ratio scale

A ratio scale has equal units and a true zero. Weight, height, age, reaction time, distance, and income are ratio variables. A value of zero represents absence of the measured quantity, so ratios are meaningful. A person weighing 80 kg is twice as heavy as a person weighing 40 kg.

5. A descriptive workflow for choosing summaries

A good descriptive analysis follows a sequence:

  1. Identify the observational unit.
  2. Identify each variable.
  3. Classify the variable as categorical or numerical.
  4. If categorical, decide whether it is nominal or ordinal.
  5. If numerical, decide whether it is discrete or continuous.
  6. Choose summaries and graphs that match the data type.
  7. Write a sentence that describes only what the data show.
Common descriptive mistake

Do not write an inferential claim when your analysis is only descriptive. “In this sample, 38% were smokers” is descriptive. “Smoking is common among adults in the city” is inferential unless the sampling design supports that claim.

Descriptive conclusion
  • Classifying variables is not a small technical detail; it is the foundation of correct description.
  • Counts and percentages belong naturally to categorical variables.
  • Means and standard deviations belong naturally to numerical variables, especially when distributions are reasonably symmetric.
  • Ordinal variables require special care because they contain order but not guaranteed equal spacing.

Practice exercises

Types of data and measurement scales

Attempt each exercise before revealing the answer.

Exercise 1
Classify variables
Easy+

Classify each variable as nominal, ordinal, discrete numerical, or continuous numerical: blood group, pain severity, number of hospital admissions, fasting glucose, treatment arm, satisfaction rating.

Answer
  • Blood group: nominal.
  • Pain severity: ordinal.
  • Number of hospital admissions: discrete numerical.
  • Fasting glucose: continuous numerical.
  • Treatment arm: nominal.
  • Satisfaction rating: ordinal, unless explicitly treated as a numerical score with justification.
Exercise 2
Choose suitable summaries
Easy+

A dataset contains smoking status with categories never, former, current. What descriptive summaries and graph would you use?

Answer

Smoking status is categorical. The natural summaries are counts and percentages in each category. A bar chart is suitable. If the categories are ordered by exposure history, present them in a meaningful order: never, former, current.

Exercise 3
Why averages can be wrong
Medium+

A researcher codes treatment group as 1 = placebo, 2 = low dose, 3 = high dose. She reports the mean treatment group as 2.1. Explain why this is not a good descriptive summary.

Answer

The numbers are codes, not real measurements. The average 2.1 does not represent an actual group and is hard to interpret. A frequency table with counts and percentages in placebo, low dose, and high dose is more appropriate. If dose order matters, the variable may be ordinal, but counts and percentages are still the first descriptive summary.

Exercise 4
Interval or ratio?
Medium+

Explain the difference between interval and ratio variables using Celsius temperature and body weight.

Answer

Celsius temperature is interval: equal differences are meaningful, but zero Celsius is not the absence of temperature, so ratios are not meaningful. Body weight is ratio: equal differences are meaningful and zero represents absence of weight, so saying 80 kg is twice 40 kg is meaningful.

Exercise 5
Build a descriptive table
Hard+

A sample of 12 patients has disease stage: I, II, II, III, I, IV, II, III, II, I, III, II. Construct a descriptive summary.

Answer

Disease stage is ordinal. Counts: I = 3, II = 5, III = 3, IV = 1. Percentages: I = 25.0%, II = 41.7%, III = 25.0%, IV = 8.3%. The modal category is stage II. Because the variable is ordinal, present categories in order from I to IV and use an ordered bar chart if plotting.
