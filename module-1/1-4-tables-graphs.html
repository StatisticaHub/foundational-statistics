Tables, graphs and data display

Mr. R · Emma · Oliver · James · Sophia · ~22 min · Descriptive statistics focus
📍 Scene — a classroom with a messy spreadsheet on the projector
EM
Emma

I have a dataset with 80 rows and 6 variables. I can calculate numbers, but it still feels like I cannot “see” the data.

MR
Mr. R

That is exactly why tables and graphs exist. A dataset is not automatically understandable just because it is stored in rows and columns. Data display turns raw observations into visible structure.

OL
Oliver

So a graph is just a nicer version of a table?

MR
Mr. R

Not quite. A table is best when exact values matter. A graph is best when pattern, comparison, shape, trend, or unusual observations matter. They answer different descriptive questions.

Why display data before summarising?

Before calculating means, standard deviations, correlations, or tests, we should ask: what does the data look like? A display can reveal outliers, impossible values, missing categories, skewness, clusters, and coding mistakes.

Key principle

Never trust a single summary number before looking at the data. The same mean can come from very different distributions.

JA
James

If I have exam scores, should I always make a histogram?

MR
Mr. R

If the scores are numerical, a histogram is a strong first choice. It shows the distribution: where most values lie, whether the data are symmetric or skewed, and whether there are possible outliers.

SO
Sophia

But for blood groups, a histogram would not make sense, right?

MR
Mr. R

Correct. Blood group is categorical, so we usually use a frequency table or a bar chart. The graph must match the variable type.

The basic matching rule

Categorical ordinalOrdered frequency tableOrdered bar chartHow do responses move across ordered levels?

Graphs are arguments

A graph does not merely decorate an analysis. It makes an argument about what the viewer should notice. This means graph design has ethical responsibility: axes, scales, labels, ordering, and omitted data can change interpretation.

EM
Emma

So a misleading graph is not always mathematically wrong. It can be visually wrong?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. A bar chart with a cut-off y-axis can exaggerate a small difference. A pie chart with too many slices can hide the important categories. A line graph for unordered categories can imply a trend that does not exist.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • Tables communicate exact values; graphs communicate visual patterns.
  • The correct display depends mainly on the variable type and the research question.
  • Frequency tables and bar charts are natural for categorical variables.
  • Histograms and boxplots are natural for numerical variables.
  • Good data display is part of statistical thinking, not decoration.
Detailed notes

Tables, graphs and data display

1. What is data display?

Data display is the process of organising observed values into tables, diagrams, and graphs so that the structure of the data becomes visible. In descriptive statistics, the goal is not to prove a claim about a population, but to understand and communicate the information contained in the observed data.

Definition — data display

A set of descriptive methods used to present data clearly, reveal patterns, detect irregularities, and support interpretation before formal inference.

2. Why raw data is not enough

Raw data is usually stored as a matrix: rows represent observational units and columns represent variables. Although this structure is useful for storage, it is not ideal for human understanding. A raw table of 500 observations may contain all information, but it does not immediately reveal whether values cluster, whether categories are imbalanced, or whether there are errors.

Good descriptive display helps answer questions such as:

  • How many observations are in each category?
  • Which value or interval is most common?
  • Are the values symmetric, skewed, clustered, or spread out?
  • Are there unusual observations that need checking?
  • Do two variables appear to move together?
  • Are groups similar or different in their distributions?

3. Frequency tables for categorical data

A frequency table counts how many observations fall into each category. It is the simplest and most important display for categorical variables. The absolute count is called the frequency. The proportion or percentage is called the relative frequency.

Relative frequency = category frequency / total frequency
Percentage = (category frequency / total frequency) × 100

A3434/100 = 0.3434%

This table is descriptive. It says what was observed in these 100 people. It does not automatically say that 41% of the wider population has blood group O unless the sample was designed to support inference.

4. Frequency tables for ordinal data

For ordinal data, the order of categories matters. Therefore, the table should preserve the natural order rather than sorting alphabetically or by frequency.

The cumulative percentage is especially useful for ordered categories because it tells us the proportion at or below a certain level. In this example, 50% of patients reported none or mild pain.

5. Grouped frequency tables for numerical data

Numerical variables can take many possible values. Listing every unique value may be unhelpful, especially for continuous measurements such as height, cholesterol, or reaction time. We often group values into class intervals.

The choice of intervals affects interpretation. Very wide intervals hide detail; very narrow intervals create noise. A good grouping balances clarity and detail.

6. Bar charts

A bar chart displays categorical frequencies using separated bars. The gaps between bars matter: they signal that the categories are distinct rather than continuous intervals. Bar charts are appropriate for nominal and ordinal variables.

Good use

Use a bar chart to compare counts or percentages across categories such as blood group, degree programme, smoking status, or satisfaction level.

Common mistake

Do not use a line chart for unordered categories. Joining categories with a line can falsely imply a continuous progression.

7. Histograms

A histogram displays the distribution of a numerical variable by grouping values into intervals called bins. Unlike a bar chart, the bars usually touch because the intervals are adjacent parts of a numerical scale.

A histogram helps describe:

  • Centre: where the data tend to cluster.
  • Spread: how widely values vary.
  • Shape: whether the distribution is symmetric, skewed, unimodal, bimodal, or irregular.
  • Outliers: values far away from the main body of data.
Bin width matters

A histogram is not unique. The same data can look smoother or noisier depending on the number of bins. Always choose bins that reveal structure without manufacturing false patterns.

8. Boxplots

A boxplot summarises a numerical distribution using the median, quartiles, and potential outliers. It is especially useful for comparing a numerical variable across groups.

IQR = Q3 − Q1
Lower fence = Q1 − 1.5 × IQR,   Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5 × IQR

The box shows the middle 50% of the data. The line inside the box is the median. Points beyond the fences are often marked as potential outliers. They are not automatically errors, but they deserve attention.

9. Scatter plots

A scatter plot displays the relationship between two numerical variables. Each point represents one observational unit. The horizontal axis usually shows the explanatory or predictor variable, and the vertical axis shows the response or outcome variable.

Scatter plots reveal direction, strength, curvature, clusters, and unusual points. A positive association means larger x-values tend to accompany larger y-values. A negative association means larger x-values tend to accompany smaller y-values.

Descriptive caution

A scatter plot can show association, but association alone does not prove causation. The graph describes the pattern; causal interpretation requires design and assumptions.

10. Line graphs

A line graph is useful when the horizontal axis has a meaningful order, especially time. It is appropriate for displaying trends in values measured over days, months, years, or ordered experimental conditions.

Line graphs should not be used merely because they look clean. If the x-axis categories have no natural sequence, a line graph may falsely suggest continuity or trend.

12. Principles of clear statistical graphics

  1. Use an informative title. The title should say what the graph shows, not merely name the graph type.
  2. Label axes clearly. Include units, such as years, kg, mmHg, or percentage.
  3. Start bar chart axes at zero. Cutting the axis can visually exaggerate differences.
  4. Keep category order meaningful. Ordinal categories should follow their natural order.
  5. Avoid unnecessary decoration. Three-dimensional effects, shadows, and excessive colours can distract from the data.
  6. Use percentages carefully. Always make clear the denominator.
  7. Show sample size where possible. A percentage based on 8 people is very different from a percentage based on 800 people.

13. Descriptive writing from tables and graphs

A good descriptive sentence should name the variable, the group, the main pattern, and the relevant numerical evidence. It should avoid overclaiming beyond the data.

Good descriptive sentence

In the sample of 100 participants, blood group O was the most common category, observed in 41 participants (41%), followed by blood group A in 34 participants (34%).

Overclaiming

Blood group O is the most common blood group in the population. This statement moves beyond the observed sample and requires inferential justification.

14. Common display mistakes

MistakeWhy it is a problemBetter choice

Summary
  • Tables and graphs are descriptive tools for understanding observed data.
  • Frequency tables and bar charts belong naturally with categorical variables.
  • Histograms, boxplots, scatter plots and line graphs belong naturally with numerical variables in different situations.
  • The display should be chosen according to the question, not personal taste.
  • Good descriptive writing reports what was observed without overclaiming.

Exercise 2
Choosing a graph
Easy+

Choose the best graph for each variable: (a) blood group, (b) exam score, (c) daily number of hospital admissions over 30 days, (d) height and weight measured on the same people.

Answer
  1. Blood group: bar chart because it is nominal categorical.
  2. Exam score: histogram or boxplot because it is numerical.
  3. Daily admissions over time: line graph because the x-axis is time.
  4. Height and weight: scatter plot because both variables are numerical and paired.
Exercise 3
Reading a grouped table
Medium+

A grouped frequency table for systolic blood pressure is: 100–119: 9, 120–139: 21, 140–159: 13, 160–179: 7. The total is 50. Calculate the percentage in each interval and describe the distribution.

Answer

Percentages: 100–119 = 18%, 120–139 = 42%, 140–159 = 26%, 160–179 = 14%. The largest group is 120–139 mmHg. The distribution has more observations in the lower-middle intervals, with fewer observations at the high end.

Exercise 4
Identifying misleading displays
Medium+

A bar chart compares pass rates of two schools: 82% and 86%. The y-axis starts at 80% instead of 0%. Explain why this may be misleading and suggest a correction.

Answer

Starting the y-axis at 80% visually exaggerates the 4 percentage point difference. A corrected bar chart should usually start at 0%, or the display should clearly state that the axis is truncated. A table may be better if the exact small difference is the main point.

Exercise 5
Detailed descriptive reporting
Hard+

A sample of 60 patients has pain severity: none 6, mild 19, moderate 24, severe 11. Write a full descriptive paragraph including counts, percentages, ordering, and a cautious interpretation.

Answer

In the sample of 60 patients, moderate pain was the most frequently reported category, with 24 patients (40%). Mild pain was reported by 19 patients (31.7%), severe pain by 11 patients (18.3%), and no pain by 6 patients (10%). Because pain severity is ordinal, the categories should be displayed in the order none, mild, moderate, severe. Overall, most observed patients reported at least mild pain, but this statement describes only the sampled patients unless the sampling design supports wider inference.
