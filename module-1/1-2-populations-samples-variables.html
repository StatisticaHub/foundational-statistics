Populations, samples & variables

Mr. R · Emma · Oliver · James · Sophia · ~16 min · Foundation level
📍 Scene — the same classroom, now with a small health survey on the board
EM
Emma

Last time we said statistics helps us learn from data. But when I open a dataset, I mostly see rows and columns. Where do populations and samples fit into that?

MR
Mr. R

Good question. Every dataset is a small window into a bigger world. The bigger world is the population. The window you actually observe is the sample. The columns you measure are variables.

OL
Oliver

So if we measure 80 patients in a clinic, the 80 patients are the sample?

MR
Mr. R

Exactly. But the key question is: what larger population do those 80 patients represent? All patients in that clinic? All patients in the city? All patients with the same condition? Your answer changes the meaning of the whole study.

The population is the target

A population is not always “everyone in the world”. It is the full group you want your conclusion to be about. In statistics, defining the population is a scientific decision, not just a mathematical formality.

Population

The complete set of individuals, objects, events, measurements, or units about which we want to make a statement.

JA
James

If I study 500 university students, can I say my population is all students in the country?

MR
Mr. R

Only if those 500 students were chosen in a way that reasonably represents all students in the country. Otherwise, your sample may describe your own university well, but not the whole country.

The sample is what we observe

A sample is the part of the population that enters our dataset. It may be large or small, carefully selected or convenient, balanced or biased. The quality of a sample is often more important than its size.

Sample

A subset of the population from which data are actually collected. Statistics computed from the sample are used to describe the sample and, when appropriate, infer something about the population.

Variables are the things we measure

Each column in a dataset usually represents a variable. A variable is a characteristic that can take different values for different observational units.

Dataset rowObservational unitPossible variables
One patientPatientAge, sex, blood pressure, diagnosis, treatment response
One schoolSchoolSchool type, average score, number of teachers, region
One country-yearCountry in a specific yearGDP, life expectancy, unemployment rate, disease incidence
SO
Sophia

So rows are units, columns are variables, and the sample is the whole collection of rows?

MR
Mr. R

Yes. That is the basic structure of most statistical datasets: rows are observational units, columns are variables, and the dataset is the sample we actually observe.

Why this matters

Many statistical mistakes happen before any calculation is performed. If the population is unclear, the sample is biased, or the variables are poorly defined, even a correct formula can produce a misleading answer.

Important idea

The same dataset can answer different questions depending on how the population, sample, unit of observation, and variables are defined.

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • The population is the full group we want to understand.
  • The sample is the part of the population we actually observe.
  • Rows usually represent observational units; columns usually represent variables.
  • A variable must be defined carefully before it can be described or analysed.
  • Good descriptive statistics begin with clear definitions of units and variables.
Detailed descriptive notes

Populations, samples & variables

1. Why this topic belongs to descriptive statistics

Before we calculate a mean, draw a histogram, build a table, or compare two groups, we must know exactly what is being described. Descriptive statistics is not only about formulas. It begins with the structure of the data: the population, the sample, the observational units, and the variables.

Descriptive statistics perspective

Descriptive statistics asks: What data do we have, what do the rows represent, what do the columns measure, and how can these observations be summarised clearly without making unsupported generalisations?

2. Population: the group of interest

A population is the complete collection of units that we are interested in. In descriptive work, we may or may not generalise to the population, but we must still state the population clearly because it gives meaning to the sample.

Population

The full set of individuals, objects, events, institutions, measurements, or time points that are relevant to the question being studied.

Examples of populations include:

  • all first-year university students at a particular university;
  • all patients diagnosed with hypertension in a hospital system during 2025;
  • all secondary schools in England;
  • all daily temperature readings from a city over ten years;
  • all households in a national census.
Descriptive caution

Do not define the population after seeing the results. The target group should be defined before description and analysis, otherwise the interpretation becomes vague and potentially misleading.

3. Sample: the observed part of the population

A sample is the subset of the population for which data are actually available. In a spreadsheet, the sample is often represented by the full set of rows. Each row is usually one observational unit.

Sample size = n = number of observed units

For example, if a researcher records age, sex, BMI, and blood pressure for 120 patients, then the sample size is:

n = 120

Descriptive statistics can summarise this sample directly. For instance, we may report the sample mean age, the number of males and females, the distribution of BMI, and the range of blood pressure values. These statements describe the observed sample. They do not automatically prove what is true in the population.

TermMeaningDescriptive example
PopulationFull group of interestAll adults registered at a GP practice

4. Observational units: what each row represents

The observational unit is the basic object on which measurements are taken. A common beginner mistake is to confuse variables with units. For example, “blood pressure” is not an observational unit; it is a variable measured on a patient.

Observational unit

The entity described by one row of the dataset. It may be a person, patient, school, household, country, event, trial, plant, cell, or time period.

Study questionLikely observational unitExample variables
How do patients differ in blood pressure?PatientAge, sex, systolic BP, diastolic BP
How do schools differ in exam performance?SchoolSchool type, region, mean score, class size
How has inflation changed over time?Month or yearInflation rate, unemployment, interest rate
How do countries differ in life expectancy?CountryLife expectancy, GDP, health spending

5. Variables: characteristics that vary

A variable is a characteristic that can take different values across observational units. The word “variable” is important because statistical description depends on variation. If everyone has exactly the same value, there is little to describe statistically.

Variable

A measured or recorded characteristic of an observational unit that can differ from unit to unit.

Examples:

  • Age varies from person to person.
  • Blood pressure varies across patients.
  • Degree programme varies across students.
  • Income group varies across households.
  • Disease status varies between affected and unaffected individuals.

6. Variable values and data entries

A variable is the column name or concept; a value is the actual entry for one unit. For example, sex is a variable, while female is a value. Age is a variable, while 21 is a value.

7. Types of variables from a descriptive viewpoint

Different variable types require different descriptive summaries. A mean is meaningful for age or blood pressure, but not for eye colour or degree programme. A bar chart is useful for categories, while a histogram is useful for continuous measurements.

Variable typeDescriptionExamplesGood descriptive tools
Categorical nominalCategories with no natural orderSex, blood group, treatment armCounts, percentages, bar charts
Categorical ordinalCategories with a natural orderPain severity, satisfaction level, cancer stageOrdered counts, percentages, stacked bars
Discrete numericalNumerical countsNumber of children, clinic visits, hospital admissionsFrequency tables, bar charts, mean/median
Continuous numericalMeasurements on a scaleHeight, weight, blood pressure, timeMean, SD, median, IQR, histogram, boxplot
Important descriptive rule

The summary must match the variable type. Reporting the mean of a nominal category is usually meaningless, while reporting only counts for a continuous variable may hide important distributional features.

8. Describing categorical variables

Categorical variables are described using frequencies and percentages. If a sample contains n observations and a category appears f times, then:

Percentage in category = (f / n) × 100

For example, if 42 out of 120 patients are current smokers, the percentage of current smokers is:

(42 / 120) × 100 = 35%

A descriptive sentence would be: “In this sample of 120 patients, 42 patients (35%) were current smokers.” Notice the phrase in this sample. That phrase keeps the statement descriptive.

9. Describing numerical variables

Numerical variables are often described using measures of centre and spread. Centre tells us where the data are concentrated. Spread tells us how variable the observations are.

Descriptive featureCommon summariesWhat it tells us
CentreMean, medianTypical or central value
SpreadRange, standard deviation, IQRHow far values differ from each other
ShapeSkewness, histogram shapeWhether the distribution is symmetric or skewed
Unusual valuesOutliers, extreme valuesWhether some observations are unusually high or low

For a numerical variable such as systolic blood pressure, a strong descriptive summary may say:

Example descriptive summary

Among the 120 patients in the sample, systolic blood pressure had a mean of 132.4 mmHg, a standard deviation of 14.8 mmHg, a median of 130 mmHg, and a range from 98 to 178 mmHg.

10. Why descriptive statistics must avoid overclaiming

Descriptive statistics tells us what was observed. It does not, by itself, tell us whether the sample represents a population, whether a treatment caused an effect, or whether the result would be repeated in another study.

Descriptive statementOverclaiming versionWhy the second is risky
In this sample, the mean age was 29.6 years.The population mean age is 29.6 years.The sample may not represent the population perfectly.
In these patients, 35% were current smokers.35% of all patients are smokers.This generalises beyond the observed data.
The treatment group had a lower mean BP in this dataset.The treatment lowers blood pressure.Causal and inferential evidence is needed.

11. A complete descriptive checklist

Before producing descriptive statistics, ask these questions:

  1. What is the research question?
  2. What is the target population?
  3. What is the sample?
  4. What does one row represent?
  5. What are the variables?
  6. What type is each variable?
  7. What values or categories are possible?
  8. Are there missing, impossible, or unusual values?
  9. Which summary is appropriate for each variable?
  10. Can the final statement be written without overgeneralising?
Descriptive notes summary
  • Population, sample, unit, variable, and value are the building blocks of descriptive statistics.
  • Rows usually represent observational units; columns usually represent variables.
  • Categorical variables are described using counts and percentages.
  • Numerical variables are described using centre, spread, shape, and unusual values.
  • Descriptive statements should stay close to the observed data unless inferential methods are used.

Practice exercises

Populations, samples & variables

Five exercises focused on identifying populations, samples, units, variables, and descriptive summaries.

Exercise 1
Population, sample and unit
Easy+

A researcher studies 180 MSc students at one university to describe their weekly study time. Identify: (a) the sample, (b) a possible population, (c) the observational unit, and (d) one variable.

Answer
  1. The sample is the 180 MSc students who were studied.
  2. A possible population is all MSc students at that university. A wider population such as all MSc students in the UK would require stronger sampling justification.
  3. The observational unit is one MSc student.
  4. One variable is weekly study time, measured in hours.
Exercise 2
Rows and columns
Easy+

A dataset contains 75 rows and 8 columns. Each row is a patient. The columns are age, sex, BMI, smoking status, systolic blood pressure, cholesterol, treatment group, and outcome. How many observational units are there? How many variables are there? Give two categorical and two numerical variables.

Answer

There are 75 observational units, because each row is one patient. There are 8 variables, because there are 8 columns. Categorical variables include sex, smoking status, treatment group, and outcome. Numerical variables include age, BMI, systolic blood pressure, and cholesterol.

Exercise 3
Choosing descriptive summaries
Medium+

For each variable, choose an appropriate descriptive summary: (a) blood group, (b) age, (c) pain severity recorded as mild/moderate/severe, (d) number of hospital visits in one year.

Answer
  1. Blood group: counts and percentages, because it is nominal categorical.
  2. Age: mean and SD if roughly symmetric; median and IQR if skewed; histogram or boxplot for distribution.
  3. Pain severity: ordered counts and percentages, because it is ordinal categorical.
  4. Hospital visits: frequency table, median/IQR, and possibly mean; it is a discrete numerical count and may be skewed.
Exercise 4
Writing careful descriptive statements
Medium+

In a sample of 200 adults, 58 report current smoking. Write a careful descriptive statement and calculate the percentage.

Answer

Percentage = (58 / 200) × 100 = 29%.

A careful descriptive statement is: “In this sample of 200 adults, 58 adults (29%) reported current smoking.” This is better than saying “29% of adults smoke,” because that would generalise beyond the observed sample.

Exercise 5
Designing a descriptive table
Hard+

You are given a dataset of 300 patients with age, sex, ethnicity, BMI, diabetes status, systolic blood pressure, and number of GP visits in the last year. Propose a descriptive Table 1 structure. Say which summaries you would use for each variable.

Answer

A strong descriptive Table 1 could include:

  • Age: mean (SD) or median (IQR), depending on distribution.
  • Sex: n (%).
  • Ethnicity: n (%) for each category, with missing values reported if present.
  • BMI: mean (SD) if approximately symmetric; median (IQR) if skewed.
  • Diabetes status: n (%).
  • Systolic blood pressure: mean (SD), plus range if useful.
  • Number of GP visits: median (IQR) and/or frequency categories, because counts are often skewed.

The table should clearly state the sample size, for example: “Characteristics of the 300 patients included in the descriptive analysis.”
