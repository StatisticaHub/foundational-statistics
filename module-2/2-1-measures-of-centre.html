Measures of centre

OL

I thought the mean was the proper average. Why do we need the others?

The big idea

A measure of centre tries to answer a simple but important descriptive question: where is the data concentrated? It reduces many observations into one representative value. But the word “representative” is delicate: different datasets require different measures.

Measure of centre

A numerical summary that describes a central, typical, or common value in a dataset. The main measures are the mean, median, and mode.

Mean, median, and mode in conversation

JA
James

So the mean uses every value. That sounds better because it uses all the information.

MR
Mr. R

Good point. The mean does use every value, and that is its strength. But it is also its weakness, because one extreme value can pull the mean away from the bulk of the data.

SO
Sophia

Then the median is the middle value, so it ignores the extreme value?

MR
Mr. R

It does not ignore the data completely, but it depends only on position after ordering the values. That makes it resistant to outliers and often better for skewed variables such as income, rent, waiting time, and hospital length of stay.

A small example

Suppose five patients wait the following number of minutes before seeing a doctor:

12, 14, 15, 16, 73

The mean is:

x̄ = (12 + 14 + 15 + 16 + 73) / 5 = 26

The median is 15, because 15 is the middle value after ordering. In this example, the mean says “26 minutes”, but four out of five patients waited 16 minutes or less. The median gives a better description of the typical patient.

Interpretation warning

The mean is not automatically the “best” measure of centre. It is best when the distribution is reasonably symmetric and extreme values are not dominating the summary.

Weighted centre

Sometimes not all observations contribute equally. If a final grade is made from coursework and an exam, the overall centre is not a simple mean of the two marks unless they have equal weights.

Weighted mean = Σ(wᵢxᵢ) / Σwᵢ

Key takeaways from this lecture
  • The mean balances the data but is sensitive to outliers.
  • The median describes the middle position and is robust to extreme values.
  • The mode gives the most common value or category.
  • The appropriate measure of centre depends on variable type and distribution shape.
  • For skewed data, report the median and often the interquartile range.
Detailed notes

Measures of centre

1. Why centre matters in descriptive statistics

Descriptive statistics begins by reducing a collection of observations into summaries that communicate the main features of the data. A measure of centre is one of the most important summaries because it describes where the data are concentrated. However, centre is not a physical location. It is a statistical concept that depends on how the data are distributed and what kind of variable is being summarised.

For example, the “centre” of a set of exam scores may mean the arithmetic balance point, the middle student after ranking, or the most commonly occurring score. These can be different numbers, especially when the distribution is skewed, has outliers, or contains repeated values.

Core descriptive question

When we calculate a measure of centre, we are asking: what value gives a useful one-number description of the typical, central, or most common observation in this dataset?

2. The arithmetic mean

The arithmetic mean, usually called the mean, is the sum of all observations divided by the number of observations. For a sample of size n with observations x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ, the sample mean is:

x̄ = (x₁ + x₂ + ··· + xₙ) / n = (1/n)Σxᵢ

The mean is the balancing point of the data. If each observation were placed on a number line with equal weight, the mean would be the point at which the number line balances. This interpretation helps explain why the mean is affected by extreme values: a very large observation pulls the balance point toward itself.

Worked example

For the dataset 4, 6, 7, 9, 9, the mean is x̄ = (4 + 6 + 7 + 9 + 9)/5 = 35/5 = 7.

Properties of the mean

  • The mean uses every observation in the dataset.
  • The mean is unique: a dataset has exactly one arithmetic mean.
  • The sum of deviations from the mean is always zero: Σ(xᵢ − x̄) = 0.
  • The mean is sensitive to outliers and skewness.
  • The mean is mathematically convenient and appears throughout inference, regression, and probability theory.

3. Derivation: why deviations from the mean sum to zero

This property is not just a computational trick. It shows that the mean is the balancing point of the data.

Proof: Σ(xᵢ − x̄) = 0
1
Σ(xᵢ − x̄)
Start with the sum of each observation's deviation from the mean.
2
Σ(xᵢ − x̄) = Σxᵢ − Σx̄
A sum of differences can be separated into the difference of sums.
3
Σx̄ = nx̄
The mean x̄ is constant, so adding it n times gives nx̄.
4
x̄ = Σxᵢ / n ⇒ nx̄ = Σxᵢ
This follows directly from the definition of the mean.
5
Σxᵢ − nx̄ = Σxᵢ − Σxᵢ = 0
Therefore the positive and negative deviations around the mean balance exactly.

4. The median

The median is the middle value when observations are arranged in increasing order. It divides the ordered dataset into two halves: approximately 50% of observations lie below the median and approximately 50% lie above it.

Definition — median

The median is the central positional value in an ordered dataset. If n is odd, it is the middle observation. If n is even, it is usually the average of the two middle observations.

Median for odd n

For the ordered data 3, 5, 6, 8, 11, there are five observations. The middle position is the third observation, so the median is 6.

Median position = (n + 1) / 2 = (5 + 1) / 2 = 3rd value

Median for even n

For the ordered data 3, 5, 6, 8, 11, 13, there are six observations. The middle lies between the third and fourth values, so the median is:

Median = (6 + 8) / 2 = 7

Why the median is robust

The median depends on the order of observations, not the magnitude of the extreme observations. If the largest value in a dataset changes from 100 to 10,000, the median may remain unchanged. This makes the median a robust measure of centre for skewed distributions.

5. The mode

The mode is the most frequently occurring value or category in a dataset. Unlike the mean and median, the mode can be used for nominal categorical variables.

Definition — mode

The mode is the value or category with the highest frequency. A dataset may have one mode, more than one mode, or no clear mode.

For example, in the dataset 2, 3, 3, 5, 6, 6, 6, 8, the mode is 6. In the categories “Never”, “Sometimes”, “Sometimes”, “Often”, “Often”, the dataset is bimodal because “Sometimes” and “Often” occur equally often.

7. Shape of distribution and centre

The relationship between mean and median gives useful information about the shape of a distribution.

  • Symmetric distribution: mean ≈ median.
  • Right-skewed distribution: mean > median because high values pull the mean upward.
  • Left-skewed distribution: mean < median because low values pull the mean downward.
Descriptive writing rule

If the mean and median are far apart, do not simply report one number. Mention the likely skewness or the influence of extreme values.

8. Weighted mean

The weighted mean is used when observations, groups, or components do not contribute equally. It is calculated by multiplying each value by its weight, summing these products, and dividing by the total weight.

Weighted mean = (Σwᵢxᵢ) / (Σwᵢ)

If weights are proportions that sum to 1, the denominator is 1 and the formula becomes:

Weighted mean = Σwᵢxᵢ
Example — final mark

A student scores 72 on coursework worth 40% and 58 on an exam worth 60%. The weighted mean is 0.40×72 + 0.60×58 = 63.6.

9. Mean from a frequency table

When data are summarised in a frequency table, the mean can be calculated by weighting each value by its frequency.

x̄ = Σ(fx) / Σf

Therefore, x̄ = 21/10 = 2.1. This is the same result we would obtain if we wrote the raw data as 1, 1, 2, 2, 2, 2, 2, 3, 3, 3.

10. Approximate mean from grouped data

Sometimes raw data are grouped into intervals, such as ages 10–19, 20–29, and 30–39. In that case, the exact values are no longer visible. We approximate the mean by using the midpoint of each class interval.

Approximate grouped mean = Σ(fm) / Σf

Here, m is the class midpoint. This gives an approximation, not an exact mean, because we do not know where the observations lie within each interval.

11. Trimmed mean

A trimmed mean is calculated after removing a fixed percentage of the smallest and largest values. It is a compromise between the mean and median: it uses many values, but reduces the influence of extremes.

For example, a 10% trimmed mean removes the lowest 10% and highest 10% of observations before calculating the mean. This is useful when data contain possible outliers but we do not want to rely only on the median.

12. Reporting centre properly

Good descriptive writing does not just list a number. It identifies the variable, sample, measure used, value, units, and any important distributional issue.

Weak reportingStronger reporting
The average was 42.The mean age of the 80 participants was 42 years.
The median was 11.The median hospital stay was 11 days, suggesting that half of patients stayed 11 days or less.
The average income was £31,000.Because income was right-skewed, the median income (£31,000) was reported as the main measure of centre.
Notes summary
  • The mean is the arithmetic balance point and is mathematically important.
  • The median is the positional centre and is robust for skewed distributions.
  • The mode is the most frequent value and is essential for categorical variables.
  • The weighted mean is necessary when observations or components have unequal importance.
  • Choice of centre must match the variable type and distribution shape.

Practice exercises

Measures of centre

Work through the questions before revealing the answers. The aim is not just calculation, but correct interpretation.

Exercise 1
Mean, median and mode from raw data
Easy+

For the dataset 6, 8, 8, 10, 13, 15, 15, 15, 20, find the mean, median and mode.

Answer

Sum = 110 and n = 9, so mean = 110/9 = 12.22. The median is the 5th ordered value, which is 13. The mode is 15 because it occurs most often. The mean is slightly below the median because the smaller values pull it down.

Exercise 2
Effect of an outlier
Easy+

A dataset of rents is: £650, £680, £700, £720, £750, £760, £2,200. Calculate the mean and median. Which better describes the typical rent?

Answer

Mean = (650 + 680 + 700 + 720 + 750 + 760 + 2200)/7 = 6460/7 = £922.86. The median is the 4th value, £720. The median better describes the typical rent because the £2,200 value is an extreme high outlier that pulls the mean upward.

Exercise 3
Weighted mean
Medium+

A module mark is calculated from assignment 1 worth 20%, assignment 2 worth 30%, and an exam worth 50%. A student scores 75, 68 and 62 respectively. Find the final weighted mean.

Answer

Weighted mean = 0.20×75 + 0.30×68 + 0.50×62 = 15 + 20.4 + 31 = 66.4. The final mark is 66.4.

Exercise 4
Mean from a frequency table
Medium+

The number of absent days in a class is summarised below: 0 days: 6 students, 1 day: 9 students, 2 days: 4 students, 3 days: 1 student. Find the mean number of absent days.

Answer

Use x̄ = Σfx/Σf. Σfx = 0×6 + 1×9 + 2×4 + 3×1 = 0 + 9 + 8 + 3 = 20. Total frequency = 6 + 9 + 4 + 1 = 20. Mean = 20/20 = 1 absent day.

Exercise 5
Proof and interpretation
Hard+

Show algebraically that Σ(xᵢ − x̄) = 0. Then explain why this does not mean that the mean is always the best descriptive centre.

Answer

Σ(xᵢ − x̄) = Σxᵢ − Σx̄ = Σxᵢ − nx̄. Since x̄ = Σxᵢ/n, we have nx̄ = Σxᵢ. Therefore Σxᵢ − nx̄ = Σxᵢ − Σxᵢ = 0. This shows that the mean is the balancing point. However, it may still be a poor description if the distribution is skewed or has outliers, because the balance point can be pulled away from most observations.
